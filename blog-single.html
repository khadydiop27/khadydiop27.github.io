    • Pronunciation


  • a (a) - Sounds like the ah sound
  • b (be) - Often sounds like a b
  • c (ce) - Before e or i, it sounds like an s. Otherwise it sounds like a k.
  • ch (che) - Sounds like the ch in cheese
  • d (de) - Sounds like the d in English; however, the tongue should be placed against upper teeth, rather than the root of your mouth. Can also sound like the th sound.
  • e (e) - Sounds like the e in elephant
  • f (efe) - Sounds like the f in English
  • g (ge) - Usually sounds like the g in English. Can also sound like a harsh h
  • h (hache) - This letter is silent
  • i (i) - Sounds like the e in the word easy.
  • j (jota) - Sounds like the English h.
  • k (ca) - Sounds like the English k. Note: This letter is uncommon in Spanish.
  • l (ele) - This letter sounds similar to the English l
  • m (eme) - Sounds like the English m.
  • n (ene) - Sounds like the English n.
  • ñ (eñe) - Sounds like the English n in the word onion.
  • o (o) - Sounds like the oa in boat, but shorter.
  • p (pe) - Sounds like the English p, but with less breath.
  • q (cu) - This letter sounds like the English k and is always followed by the letter u.
  • r (ere) - Sounds similar to the d in the word caddy.
  • rr (doble ere) - This is a trilled r.
  • s (ese) - Sounds like the English s.
  • t (te) - A soft English t.
  • u (u) - Sounds like the oo in food.
  • v (ve) - Sounds like the Spanish b
  • x (equis) - Sounds like ks in socks.
  • y (griega) - This letter mostly sounds like the y in English. Sometimes it sounds like the letter i. (ex. hay)
  • z (zeta) - Sounds like the Spanish s, but can also sound like the th sound in many parts of Spain.

    Verbal Forms

    1. Indicative Mood
    2. Subjunctive Mood
    3. Imperative Mood

    Indicative Mood

    The indicative mood is used to talk about true actions, events and states as well as facts.

    • Quiero comer comida china.
    • Vamos a la escuela.

    Subjunctive Mood

    The subjunctive mood is used to talk about desire, will, orders, emotions, reacions, negative opinions and doubt.

    • Es bueno que hayas aprendido español.
    • Dudo que podamos ir.

    Imperative Mood

    The imperative mood is used to give commands or orders.

    • No hables.
    • Ven aquí.

    Accent Marks and Stressing

    The basics: Spanish accents (tildes) can only be written over a, e, i, o and u (á, é, í, ó, ú)

    Syllables with an accent always have the stress.

    For most words that end in a consonant (besides n and s), the stress falls on the last syllable:

    • Comer (co-mer)
    • Leer (le-er)
    • El animal (a-ni-mal)

    For words that end in a vowel, n or s, the stress falls on the second to last syllable:

    • Banco (ban-co)
    • Libros (li-bros)
    • Señora (se-ño-ra)

    Tongue Twisters

    Tongue twisters in Spanish are a great way to practice oral skills!

    • Pablito clavó un clavito, ¿qué clavito clavó pablito?

    • El burro acarrea corriendo el barril, burro, burrón, carril, carrilón, sube corriendo al ferrocarril, que es mucho más fácil subirse al vagón.

    • Tres tristes tigres tragan trigo en un trigal.

    • Camarero, caramelo, camarada camarón, quiebra el vaso y tres braseros, quema brasa del carbón.

    • Pepe Pecas pica papas con un pico, con un pico pica papas Pepe Pecas.

    • Silba Silvia sin saber, silba el sabio, silba bien, que quien bien sabe silbar, sabe bien que silba bien.

    Trouble Areas

    Gender and Number Agreement

    In Spanish, adjectives must agree in gender and number with the noun (or pronoun) they describe:

    • Las faldas bonitas.
    • Los perros negros.
    • Una camisa blanca.
    • Un libro negro.

    If the adjective ends in a consonant, it only changes according to number:

    • La moto es azul.
    • El coche es azul.
    • Las chichas jóvenes están en la escuela.
    • Los chicos jóvenes están en la escuela.

    "Ser y Estar"

    In general, ser is used to talk about what something is, while estar is used to talk about how something is:


    Ser:

    • La casa es mía.
    • Soy abogada.
    • Somos mejores amigos.
    • Ellos son de Colombia.

    Estar:

    • Estoy feliz hoy.
    • La casa está limpia.
    • Los niños están muy emocionados.
    • Estamos viendo una película.

    Pronominal Verbs and Reflexive Pronouns

    Pronominal verbs require a reflexive pronoun:

    • Me levanto a las 8.
    • Se lavan las manos
    • Tú puedes vestirte.

    Uses of "se"

    The major uses of "se":

    • As a reflexive pronoun:
      • Se cepilla los dientes.
      • Se mira en el espejo.
      • Me siento en la silla.
    • With passive voice:
      • Se comió el pollo.
      • Se buscan nuevos empleados.
      • Se compran coches usados.
    • Impersonal se:
      • Se vende ropa en la tienda.
      • Se habla español en muchos países del mundo.
      • Se sugiere comer una manzana cada día.
